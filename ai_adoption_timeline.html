AI Adoption
in Practice

A look inside how one 178-year-old organization navigated AI integration, from a single grassroots experiment to an enterprise-wide governance model, across approximately two years.

Organization Large insurance company, Midwestern US
Size ~3,000 employees, two-thirds in Ohio
Starting posture Grassroots, no formal AI mandate at launch
Phase 1

The spark: one team starts experimenting

Months 1 to 6

AI adoption began not with a strategy document or executive mandate, but with a single person in the actuarial practice exploring what was possible. No formal program. No budget. Just curiosity.

Actuarial team begins informal AI experimentation
Early use cases center on personal productivity: drafting, summarizing, research
Word spreads organically across adjacent teams
No governance structure yet, adoption entirely peer-driven
Key insight "It was very organic, it started grassroots, first person was in the actuarial practice."
Phase 2

Building community: the AI incubator forms

Months 6 to 12

Interest grew quickly enough that a formal community of practice emerged as an incubator for experimentation. Two employees became fully dedicated to AI adoption. The organization brought in outside voices and created structured moments to surface what was working.

Community of practice launched, meeting every other week
IT moderator selected to facilitate cross-team learning
Pilot licenses secured for ChatGPT and GitHub Copilot
Two employees' roles shift entirely to AI enablement
AI Day and hackathon held on campus, generating early solutions
Key insight Peer learning, not formal training, drove upskilling. No structured curriculum existed; knowledge spread through community participation and experimentation.
Phase 3

Proving value: viable solutions emerge

Months 12 to 18

Experimentation began yielding real business results. Use cases moved beyond personal productivity into operational workflows, with early wins in claims handling, underwriting support, and security operations. The question shifted from "can AI help?" to "how do we scale what's working?"

Claims handling identified as top use case: AI condenses large volumes of documentation for human review
Underwriting support: AI aggregates geo, weather, and risk data to inform decisions
Security operations: AI identifies anomalies and recommends responses for analyst review
Email security: AI detects phishing patterns and sender anomalies
Human review embedded as a non-negotiable checkpoint across all production use cases
Key insight Every production use case retained a human in the loop. Legal, ethical, and trust considerations made autonomous AI decision-making a non-starter, particularly in insurance contexts.
Phase 4

Formalizing: governance and the hub-and-spoke model

Months 18 to 24

With proven value across multiple teams, the organization moved to formalize what had grown organically. A hub-and-spoke governance model emerged, with a center of excellence coordinating adoption across business lines. Oversight structures were built to address risk, regulation, and shadow AI concerns.

Hub-and-spoke model established: center of excellence as hub, business lines as spokes
AI governance review board formed: includes Chief Privacy Officer, Chief Data Officer, senior actuarial and IT leaders
AI steering committee created above the review board, presenting quarterly to the board of directors
Shadow AI formally named as a risk: "Can't protect what you're not aware of"
CEO signals AI is not optional, creating organization-wide permission to engage
Cascading goal introduced: every employee identifies at least one AI-supported optimization opportunity
Key insight Governance followed learning, not the other way around. The structures that emerged were shaped by two years of lived experience, not by theoretical planning.
Current state

Scaling: from productivity to enterprise integration

Now

The organization describes itself as in the "vast follower plus" category, past early adoption but not yet a market shaper. The focus is shifting from personal productivity toward embedding AI into core business processes, while managing the cultural and organizational change that comes with it.

Approximately 90% of AI use still in personal productivity mode
Community of practice now 500+ members on a dedicated Teams channel
New bridge roles emerging to connect business and technical teams
Open question: how to scale what works in one business line across the enterprise
Org change management identified as the next major challenge
Concern about new hires missing formative developmental experiences as AI shortcuts learning
Key insight "No one is an expert in AI." The organization is not waiting for expertise to arrive. It is building the capacity to learn, adapt, and govern as it goes.
Horizon

The agentic future: AI orchestrates workflows autonomously

Looking ahead

Leadership anticipates a near-future in which agentic AI, systems capable of acting independently across tasks, replaces today's tool-by-tool model. Security operations offer a preview: rather than routing alerts through multiple systems, all log data flows into a security lake where AI organically correlates and responds.

Agentic AI to orchestrate security operations without manual alert routing
Shift from third-party SaaS products toward custom-built AI applications
New team structures and skill mixes required as roles evolve
Governance question: who is accountable when AI acts without a human checkpoint?
Open question The acceleration is "unbelievable." The organizations that will lead are not those with the best tools, but those that build the human capacity, trust, and governance to keep pace.
The through line

AI readiness is not a destination. It is a two-year journey of curiosity, community, proven value, and earned governance, built one experiment at a time by people who were willing to not know the answer yet.
