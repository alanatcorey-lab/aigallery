ORL 5199 · Augmented by Design

AI Best
Practices

Five research-grounded practices for leaders navigating AI integration — drawn from course frameworks and the evidence on what actually works.

1
Learning culture

Build a learning culture before buying AI tools

The principle

Technology without culture is infrastructure without people. Organizations that invest in tools before building the conditions for learning find adoption stalls — not because the tools fail, but because teams aren't ready to experiment, make mistakes, or share what they discover.

What research tells us

Chamorro-Premuzic & Bersin identify four conditions that make learning cultures stick: reward learning over performance, model curiosity from leadership, create space for reflection, and normalize not knowing. Deloitte's AI-readiness research echoes this — organizations that cultivate psychological safety and peer knowledge-sharing before deploying AI tools see significantly stronger adoption outcomes.

→
Leader action

Ask your team what they're curious about — not what they've already mastered. Reward the question, not just the answer.

2
Governance

Start grassroots, then formalize governance

The principle

Waiting for a fully formed AI strategy before allowing experimentation often means waiting too long. The organizations making the most progress tend to let curiosity lead — and build governance structures around what they learn, not the other way around.

What research tells us

Kolbjørnsrud's six principles for intelligent organizations emphasize that distributed experimentation — letting teams explore AI applications close to the work — produces richer insight than centralized planning. Over time, communities of practice become the connective tissue between grassroots learning and organizational policy. Frimpong & Wolfs find that adaptive leaders who support emergent adoption are better positioned to scale it responsibly.

→
Leader action

Create a low-stakes space for teams to experiment. Document what works. Let governance follow evidence, not assumption.

3
Human oversight

Keep humans in the loop for high-stakes decisions

The principle

AI can process information at a scale and speed no human can match — but speed and scale are not the same as accountability. In consequential decisions, removing the human from the loop doesn't just risk error; it risks eroding the trust that organizations depend on.

What research tells us

Daugherty & Wilson's missing middle framework argues that the highest-value human contributions — ethical judgment, contextual empathy, stakeholder accountability — are precisely what AI cannot replicate. Kandasamy's ethical leadership framework adds that leaders must actively design for human oversight, particularly where AI outputs affect individuals' lives, livelihoods, or rights. Accountability cannot be delegated to an algorithm.

→
Leader action

Map your team's workflows and explicitly identify which decisions require human review — before AI is deployed, not after.

4
Psychological safety

Establish psychological safety around AI mistakes

The principle

AI will produce errors — hallucinations, biased outputs, flawed recommendations. If team members fear judgment for surfacing those mistakes, organizations lose their most important feedback loop and compound the harm.

What research tells us

The BART system of group analysis highlights how boundary and authority dynamics shape what people feel safe raising. When AI errors carry professional stigma, they go unreported. Reitz & Higgins find that teams where leaders openly model uncertainty — including about AI outputs — develop stronger norms around critical evaluation. Crawford's work adds that AI systems encode existing biases; detecting them requires teams who feel safe enough to speak up.

→
Leader action

Share an AI output that missed the mark — with your team. Model what it looks like to catch it, name it, and correct it.

5
Team norms

Create AI-specific team norms and shared expectations

The principle

When AI enters a team, existing norms — about communication, decision-making, and accountability — don't automatically adapt. Without intentional norm-setting, teams default to inconsistency: some members rely heavily on AI, others avoid it, and no one is sure whose outputs to trust.

What research tells us

The GRPI model frames effective teams as those with clarity across four dimensions: goals, roles, processes, and interpersonal norms. AI disrupts all four simultaneously. Katzenbach & Smith's discipline of teams research shows that high-performing teams establish explicit working agreements — and AI integration requires a new layer of those agreements: When do we use AI? Who reviews its outputs? How do we handle disagreement with what it produces? Columbia Business School's research finds that teams who set these norms proactively outperform those who let usage patterns emerge by default.

→
Leader action

Facilitate a team conversation — not a policy — about how AI fits into your workflows. Co-create the norms; don't hand them down.
